External Radiative Efficiency (ERE) Quantification

Developed a MATLAB-based simulation platform for calculating the ERE in organic photovoltaics (OPV)  
The primary research topic focuses on accurately quantifying the non-radiation losses, particularly by evaluating the ERE of photovoltaic devices based on Planck’s Law and Schockley-Queisser limit.
Through theorical modeling and simulations, this work investigates the origins of efficiency losses and examines the relationship between solar cell performance and calculated ERE value.
Future Outlook: a predictive model to automate the screening of existing PV device databases, efficiently forecasting luminescent performance and ERE values.

Nanophotonic Design in OPVs

Nanophotonic Design Enhancing OPV Efficiency   
By employing the Purcell effect, this work pioneered the demonstration of a mechanism to boost radiative efficiency, achieving a photoluminescence quantum yield (PLQY) exceeding 20%.
This breakthrough provided the critical experimental foundation for overcoming the measurement bottleneck of device external radiative efficiency (ERE).
To streamline the process, Design of Experiments (DoE) methodology is introduced to systematically screen parameters for PLQY optimization, significantly enhancing both experimental efficiency and scientific reliability.

Photophysical Studies for Organic Semiconductors

Designed and built, both steady-state and transient (PL/TRPL) characterization, optical measurement system   
Steady-state optical properties were investigated using photoluminescence (PL) spectroscopy and PL quantum yield (PLQY) measurements to evaluate the macroscale radiative efficiency enhancement.
Time-resolved measurements were performed using two complementary techniques: ultrafast spectroscopy (PicoQuant system) for lifetime measurements and femtosecond-resolved PL spectroscopy (collaboration with Prof. Piotrowiak's group at Rutgers-Newark) to analyze exciton dynamics at the picosecond timescale, particularly focusing on triplet state transfer processes.

Photonic Integrated Circuits (PICs) for Optical Communication

Lithium Niobate Optical Modulator Process Development   
@ Nokia-Bell Labs, Optoelectronic Co-ops   
Lithium niobate's strong Pockels effect, its refractive index varies in response to an applied electric field, makes it an ideal material for optical modulators.
Developed diamond-like carbon (DLC) as a hard mask to address the challenges of etching high-hardness lithium niobate materials.
By using PE-CVD, DLC was developed and optimized, followed by systematic characterization that led to an optimized reactive ion etching (RIE) process with near-vertical (≈86°) sidewalls.

Project of Interested: COMSOL for Waveguide

COMSOL simulation of Etching Profile on Waveguide   
The fabrication of waveguides in high-hardness materials like, LN, often lead to over-etched, shallow ridge waveguide.
COMSOL Multiphysics is used to simulate the optical performance of two common etch profiles—60° and 90° (rectangular)—in a silicon-on-insulator platform.
Simulations confirm that 90° waveguide provides superior mode confinement, exhibiting a higher effective index (N_eff) for TE polarizations.
In contrast, the tilted 60° profile demonstrates higher propagation loss and more scattering.
